1. Unified diagram: effective skew ratio vs skew sensitivity vs threshold sensitivity

Threshold sensitivity
Main question: If I move the threshold, how does the operating point move, and how does the metric change?

This is about moving along a curve produced by one scoring classifier.
Effective skew ratio
Main question: At a fixed evaluation context, what trade-off between TPR and FPR keeps the metric constant?

This is the slope of a metric isometric in 2D ROC space.
Skew sensitivity
Main question: At a fixed ROC point, how much does the metric value change if class skew / prevalence / cost assumptions change?

This is well captured by a derivative such as \( \partial M/\partial c \).
Very short distinction.
- Threshold sensitivity: move the classifier threshold.
- Effective skew ratio: look at the shape of equal-metric lines in ROC space.
- Skew sensitivity: change the evaluation context \(c\) or prevalence while keeping the ROC point fixed.

2. Exam-style summary: the most important insights from Flach’s paper

Core message: many ML metrics can be understood geometrically by studying their isometrics in ROC space. The key hidden variable is the skew ratio, which encodes class prevalence and/or cost emphasis.
  1. ROC space is not only for plotting classifiers. Flach uses ROC space as a general tool to analyse metrics themselves.
  2. 2D ROC is only one slice of a 3D story. The third dimension is the skew ratio \(c\). Different values of \(c\) can change the meaning of a metric.
  3. Isometrics are contour lines of equal metric value. They show where the metric takes the same value in ROC space.
  4. The most important characteristic of a metric is its effective skew landscape. In practice: look at the slope of the isometric. That slope says what trade-off between TPR and FPR the metric rewards.
  5. Some metrics are skew-sensitive, some are weakly skew-insensitive, some fully skew-insensitive. Accuracy depends directly on \(c\). Precision and WRAcc are weakly skew-insensitive. Fully skew-insensitive versions can be constructed, e.g. \(TPR-FPR\) for WRAcc-like behaviour.
  6. F-measure and G-measure are skew-equivalent. They have the same effective skew ratio, so geometrically they encode the same local trade-off.
  7. F-measure is more positively biased than precision. It gives even more emphasis to the positive class than precision does.
  8. Splitting criteria can also be analysed this way. Gini-split is more skew-sensitive than information gain; DKM-split is weakly skew-insensitive.
  9. Big exam insight: a metric is not just a formula. It encodes a hidden preference about which errors matter, and ROC isometrics let you see that preference geometrically.
Exam trap: do not say “Flach just introduced another ROC curve.” What he really did was provide a geometric theory of metrics.

3. What exactly is the slope? What is the effective skew ratio?

3.1 Start from an isometric

An isometric is a set of points \((FPR,TPR)\) where a metric has the same value. For example, for accuracy:

\[ Accuracy=\frac{TPR+c(1-FPR)}{1+c} \]

Fix the metric value to \(m\):

\[ \frac{TPR+c(1-FPR)}{1+c}=m \]

Now solve for \(TPR\):

\[ TPR = m(1+c)-c(1-FPR) \]

\[ TPR = \underbrace{m(1+c)-c}_{intercept} + \underbrace{c}_{slope}\cdot FPR \]

So the isometric is a straight line in ROC space, and its slope is \(c\). That slope is Flach’s effective skew ratio for accuracy.

3.2 Intuition

The slope tells you how much extra TPR you need to compensate for an increase in FPR if you want the metric value to stay the same.

  • If the slope is large, the metric tolerates an increase in FPR only if you gain a lot in TPR.
  • If the slope is small, the metric is more forgiving about FPR increases.

3.3 Effective skew ratio as a local quantity

For some metrics, the isometrics are not all parallel. Then the slope depends on the point \((FPR,TPR)\). Flach calls this the effective skew ratio or effective skew landscape.

MetricEffective skew ratioInterpretation
Accuracy\(c\)Directly reflects expected skew / cost ratio.
Precision\(TPR/FPR\)Local slope depends on where you are in ROC space.
F-measure, G-measure\( \frac{TPR}{FPR+1/c} \)Also point-dependent; more biased toward positives.
WRAcc\(1\)Parallel to the ascending diagonal.

3.4 How are these computed?

  1. Write the metric in ROC form using TPR, FPR, and \(c\).
  2. Set the metric equal to a constant \(m\).
  3. Rearrange to get the isometric equation.
  4. Read off the slope if the line is linear; or compute the local derivative \(dTPR/dFPR\) if the isometric is nonlinear.
Example: precision

\[ Precision=\frac{TPR}{TPR+cFPR}=m \]

\[ TPR = m(TPR+cFPR) \]

\[ TPR-mTPR = mcFPR \]

\[ TPR(1-m)=mcFPR \]

\[ TPR=\frac{mc}{1-m}FPR \]

So for a fixed \(m\), the isometric is a line through the origin. Its slope is \(\frac{mc}{1-m}\). If you eliminate \(m\) using the point itself, you get the effective skew ratio \(TPR/FPR\).

Example: F1

\[ F_1=\frac{2TPR}{TPR+cFPR+1}=m \]

\[ 2TPR = m(TPR+cFPR+1) \]

\[ TPR(2-m)=m(cFPR+1) \]

\[ TPR = \frac{m(cFPR+1)}{2-m} \]

The slope depends on \(m\), and if you re-express it locally at a point you get the effective skew ratio shown by Flach.

4. Derivative / skew sensitivity: what it is and how to summarise it

This is your idea. It is different from Flach’s slope, but it is very useful.

Definition. Fix a ROC point \((FPR,TPR)\). Then look at how the metric value changes as \(c\) changes: \[ \frac{\partial M}{\partial c} \] This measures skew sensitivity of the metric value.
MetricROC/skew form\(\partial M/\partial c\)Interpretation
Accuracy\(\frac{TPR+c(1-FPR)}{1+c}\)\(\frac{(1-FPR)-TPR}{(1+c)^2}=\frac{TNR-TPR}{(1+c)^2}\)Insensitive only when \(TPR=TNR\).
Balanced accuracy\(\frac{TPR+TNR}{2}\)\(0\)Prevalence-insensitive.
Precision\(\frac{TPR}{TPR+cFPR}\)\(-\frac{TPR\cdot FPR}{(TPR+cFPR)^2}\)Usually decreases as \(c\) grows.
F1\(\frac{2TPR}{TPR+cFPR+1}\)\(-\frac{2TPR\cdot FPR}{(TPR+cFPR+1)^2}\)Also skew-sensitive.

How can we summarise skew sensitivity in one number?

Option A
Average absolute sensitivity
\[S_{avg}(M)=\mathbb{{E}}\left[\left|\frac{{\partial M}}{{\partial c}}\right|\right]\]Average over a chosen region of interest in ROC space and a chosen range of \(c\).
Option B
Worst-case sensitivity
\[S_{max}(M)=\sup \left|\frac{{\partial M}}{{\partial c}}\right|\]Useful if you care about robustness.
Option C
Integrated sensitivity
\[S_{int}(M)=\int_{{c_{{min}}}}^{{c_{{max}}}}\left|\frac{{\partial M}}{{\partial c}}\right|dc\]For one fixed ROC point, this measures total change across a skew range.
Exam answer suggestion: “Flach’s effective skew ratio describes the geometry of isometrics in ROC space. A derivative such as \(\partial M/\partial c\) describes sensitivity of the metric value to skew assumptions. The latter is not in Flach’s original formulation, but it is a natural complementary analysis.”

5. Balanced accuracy: more than “specificity + sensitivity”

\[ Balanced\ Accuracy = \frac{Sensitivity+Specificity}{2}=\frac{TPR+TNR}{2} \]

  1. Average recall of the two classes. You measure how well you recognise positives and negatives, then average them equally.
  2. Accuracy under equal class importance. It acts like ordinary accuracy after removing prevalence bias.
  3. A prevalence-insensitive alternative to accuracy. If one class is rare, normal accuracy can be misleading; balanced accuracy avoids this by giving the two classes equal weight.
So yes, you can think of it as a weighted accuracy where the weights are chosen so that each class contributes equally, regardless of class size.

But it is not the same as a general cost-sensitive accuracy. Cost-sensitive accuracy would use weights derived from the actual costs of FP and FN.

6. Skew and cost considerations from the slide

Imbalance and cost are different:

  • Imbalance says how frequent the classes are.
  • Cost says how bad each error type is.

The slide suggests weighting class-conditional performance with ratios like \(P/N\) or \(N/P\), or directly weighting off-diagonal confusion matrix entries by misclassification costs:

\[ Cost = C_{{FN}}\cdot FN + C_{{FP}}\cdot FP \]

Important idea: sometimes rare classes deserve more weight because they are rare; sometimes they deserve more weight because the errors are more costly; sometimes both; sometimes neither. Do not mix these up.

7. F\(_\alpha\), F\(_\beta\), F1, and G-mean

F-scores combine precision and recall. The standard family is

\[ F_\beta = \frac{(1+\beta^2)PR}{\beta^2P+R} \]

where \(P=Precision\), \(R=Recall\).

  • \(\beta=1\): \(F_1\), equal emphasis on precision and recall.
  • \(\beta>1\): recall is more important.
  • \(\beta<1\): precision is more important.
So: F1 = symmetric compromise, F2 = recall-oriented, F0.5 = precision-oriented.

Some slides write \(F_\alpha\) instead of \(F_\beta\), but the conceptual role is the same: tune the trade-off.

G-mean is often \(\sqrt{TPR\cdot TNR}\) or sometimes \(\sqrt{TPR\cdot Precision}\). It penalizes imbalance strongly because it is multiplicative.

8. Why does the slide call accuracy “multi-class focus”?

It means accuracy is a more global measure that looks at all classes together. Precision, recall, specificity and related measures are usually introduced with a one-class focal view.

Multi-class / global focus
Accuracy uses all correct decisions together. In multiclass form: \[ Accuracy=\frac{\sum_i TP_i}{N} \]
Single-class focus
To compute precision/recall in multiclass problems, take each class in turn as “positive” and the rest as “negative”, then aggregate.

Aggregations:

  • Macro: average across classes equally.
  • Weighted macro: average weighted by support.
  • Micro: pool TP, FP, FN over all classes first.
Exam trap: in single-label multiclass classification, micro-precision = micro-recall = micro-F1 = accuracy.

9. AUC and ROC slides explained more clearly

ROC is for a scoring classifier. As you vary the threshold, you get different \((FPR,TPR)\) pairs. Plotting them gives the ROC curve.

AUC has two famous interpretations:
  1. Average ranking performance over thresholds / operating points.
  2. Probability interpretation: the probability that a randomly chosen positive gets a higher score than a randomly chosen negative.

AUC is often more robust than accuracy under imbalance because a trivial majority-class classifier can have high accuracy but no useful ranking skill.

But ROC itself ignores prevalence. Many real performance measures depend on prevalence or cost. That is why Flach recommends plotting isometrics of the performance measure of interest on top of ROC space.

10. Short explanations of smROC, H-measure, Information score, and Efficiency Method

smROC: smoothed / stabilized ROC-style analysis; useful when the empirical ROC curve is jagged and you want a cleaner geometric picture.

H-measure: a scalar alternative to AUC that fixes a problem-level cost distribution first, so classifier comparison is cost-coherent.

Information score: a family of measures for probabilistic classifiers. Compare what you believed before prediction (the prior) with what the classifier says after seeing the features (the posterior). Good models reduce uncertainty and assign high probability to the true class.

Efficiency Method: multi-criteria evaluation. Instead of forcing everything into one scalar, compare models on several axes such as accuracy, comprehensibility, interestingness, or cost.

11. Short explanation: multi-rater / multi-class agreement

These measures are used when there is no perfect ground truth and labels come from human raters.

  • Fleiss’ kappa: generalizes Cohen’s kappa to multiple raters.
  • Scott’s pi: another chance-corrected agreement coefficient.
  • ICC: intraclass correlation, often for quantitative ratings.
  • κS / S measure: proposals to better handle fixed-rater settings.
Big picture: when labels come from experts rather than unquestioned ground truth, we may want to know how much agreement exists beyond chance.

12. What are visualization-based measures and chance-corrected evaluation about?

Visualization-based approaches treat classifier evaluation data as high-dimensional data. Accuracy or F1 are just one projection. Other projections can reveal structure, clusters, or error geometry.

Chance-corrected measures are useful when labels are noisy, biased, or imprecise. Raw accuracy may overstate performance because some agreement would occur by chance anyway.
