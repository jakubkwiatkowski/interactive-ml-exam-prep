Performance Measures — Exam Guide

Based mainly on the ICML tutorial and Flach’s ROC-isometric paper. Your derivative-with-respect-to-\(c\) idea is intentionally excluded from the exam core.

1. The evaluation map

Main principle: a metric is chosen from the evaluation question, not independently of it.
Output availableMain questionTypical measures
Hard labels at one thresholdHow good are the final decisions?Accuracy, precision, recall, specificity, F1, balanced accuracy, MCC
Scores across thresholdsHow good is ranking and threshold trade-off?ROC, AUC, PR, DET, lift, cost curves
ProbabilitiesAre numerical probabilities accurate and reliable?Brier/RMSE, log-loss, calibration, information measures
Imperfect human labelsHow much agreement exists beyond chance?Cohen’s kappa, Fleiss’ kappa, ICC
Different metrics may rank the same classifiers differently because every metric reveals some information and hides other information.

2. Derivation toolkit: remember a few bases, derive the rest

Exam strategy: do not memorize every transformed formula. Memorize the confusion-matrix definitions and the change-of-variables identities, then derive ROC/skew forms.

Base identities

\[ TP=POS\cdot TPR,\qquad FN=POS(1-TPR) \]

\[ FP=NEG\cdot FPR,\qquad TN=NEG(1-FPR) \]

\[ pos=\frac{POS}{POS+NEG},\qquad c=\frac{NEG}{POS}=\frac{1-pos}{pos},\qquad pos=\frac1{1+c} \]

Example: derive accuracy in ROC/prevalence/skew form

Start with:

\[ Accuracy=\frac{TP+TN}{POS+NEG} \]

Substitute \(TP=POS\,TPR\) and \(TN=NEG(1-FPR)\):

\[ Accuracy=\frac{POS\,TPR+NEG(1-FPR)}{POS+NEG} \]

Divide by the total to obtain prevalence form:

\[ Accuracy=pos\,TPR+(1-pos)(1-FPR) \]

Or divide numerator and denominator by POS and use \(c=NEG/POS\):

\[ Accuracy=\frac{TPR+c(1-FPR)}{1+c} \]

You only need the standard accuracy formula plus the four substitutions. The 3D formula does not need to be memorized separately.

Means as memory devices

Dice gives F1

\[ Dice=\frac{2|A\cap B|}{|A|+|B|} \]

For predicted-positive set \(A\) and true-positive set \(B\):

\[ |A\cap B|=TP,\quad |A|=TP+FP,\quad |B|=TP+FN \]

Therefore:

\[ Dice=\frac{2TP}{2TP+FP+FN}=F_1 \]

4. Core metrics: meaning, derivation, and comparison

Accuracy

Correct predictions divided by all predictions. It is a prevalence-weighted average of TPR and TNR:

\[Accuracy=pos\,TPR+(1-pos)TNR\]

Therefore the majority class has more influence when prevalence is imbalanced.

Precision versus recall

Precision conditions on the predicted-positive set; recall conditions on the true-positive set.

\[Precision=\frac{TP}{TP+FP},\qquad Recall=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}\]

Precision asks whether alarms are trustworthy. Recall asks whether positives were found.

F1 and F-beta

\[F_1=\frac{2PR}{P+R}=\frac{2TP}{2TP+FP+FN}\]

The harmonic mean is used because a high value requires both components to be high. For \(P=1,R=0.1\), arithmetic mean is 0.55, geometric mean about 0.316, and harmonic mean about 0.182.

\[F_\beta=\frac{(1+\beta^2)PR}{\beta^2P+R}\]

\(\beta>1\) emphasizes recall; \(\beta<1\) emphasizes precision.

Balanced accuracy and G-mean

\[BA=\frac{TPR+TNR}{2},\qquad G=\sqrt{TPR\cdot TNR}\]

Both are prevalence-insensitive class-balance measures. BA uses an arithmetic mean; G penalizes uneven performance more strongly because multiplication collapses when either recall is poor.

WRAcc

Weighted relative accuracy is used in subgroup discovery:

\[WRAcc=\frac{4c}{(1+c)^2}(TPR-FPR)\]

Intuitively it rewards a subgroup whose positive rate is above the population rate, while also rewarding subgroup coverage. The factor depending on \(c\) rescales the value; the geometric core is \(TPR-FPR\), so isometrics have slope 1.

Tutorial insight: each metric conveys some information and hides other information, so classifiers may be ranked differently depending on the chosen measure.

5. Class imbalance and asymmetric costs

Class frequency and error cost are distinct:

  • Prevalence: how often positives and negatives occur.
  • Cost: how serious FP and FN are.

Expected cost at one threshold:

\[ EC=P(+)C_{FN}FNR+P(-)C_{FP}FPR \]

Balanced accuracy: \[ BA=\frac{TPR+TNR}{2} \] It is ordinary classwise recall averaged with equal class weight.
Balanced accuracy removes prevalence weighting; it does not automatically encode arbitrary application costs.

Threshold optimization

For every distinct score threshold compute:

\[EC(t)=P(+)C_{FN}FNR(t)+P(-)C_{FP}FPR(t)\]

Choose:

\[t^*=\arg\min_t EC(t)\]

For a finite empirical ROC curve, the reliable algorithm is a sorted threshold sweep, not calculus. The curve is a staircase and may have corners or ties. A derivative/tangency condition is a geometric characterization for a smooth population ROC, not the normal empirical algorithm.

5. Scores, thresholds, and ROC

A scoring classifier outputs a score. A threshold converts it to a class label.

Every threshold gives one point:

\[ (FPR(t),TPR(t)) \]

Connecting threshold points gives the ROC curve.

Interpret ROC as a menu: every point is one decision policy, with one hit rate and one false-alarm rate.

The curve can be constructed efficiently by sorting scores once and sweeping through distinct score values. Complexity is mainly \(O(n\log n)\) for sorting, then \(O(n)\) for the sweep.

6. AUC and ranking

The safest interpretation:

\[ AUC=P(s(X^+)>s(X^-)) \]

AUC is the probability that a randomly selected positive receives a higher score than a randomly selected negative, with half-credit for ties.

Rank-sum form:

\[ AUC= \frac{ \sum_{i\in +}R_i-\frac{n_+(n_++1)}2 }{ n_+n_- } \]

The subtraction removes the minimum rank sum the positives would have if they occupied the lowest \(n_+\) ranks. What remains counts correctly ordered positive-negative pairs.

Trapezoidal form:

\[ AUC=\sum_i(FPR_{i+1}-FPR_i)\frac{TPR_i+TPR_{i+1}}2 \]

AUC evaluates ranking, not calibration, and may hide poor performance in the specific operating region that matters.

9. Flach: derive and interpret ROC isometrics

Expected skew and 3D coordinates

\[c=\frac{NEG}{POS}=\frac{1-pos}{pos},\qquad pos=\frac1{1+c}\]

The three coordinates are FPR, TPR, and an operating-context coordinate such as \(c\) or \(pos\). Fixing \(c\) gives one ordinary 2D ROC slice.

Isometric and effective skew

An isometric is an equal-metric contour:

\[M(FPR,TPR;c)=m\]

Along it, the metric remains constant:

\[dM=M_{FPR}\,dFPR+M_{TPR}\,dTPR=0\]

Hence:

\[s_{eff}=\frac{dTPR}{dFPR}=-\frac{M_{FPR}}{M_{TPR}}\]

This slope is the TPR increase required to compensate for a small FPR increase while preserving the metric value.

Accuracy derivation

\[\frac{TPR+c(1-FPR)}{1+c}=m\]

Rearrange:

\[TPR=m(1+c)-c+cFPR\]

The isometric is linear with slope:

\[s_{eff}=c\]

Precision derivation

\[\frac{TPR}{TPR+cFPR}=m\]

\[TPR=\frac{mc}{1-m}FPR\]

The line passes through the origin, so its slope is also \(TPR/FPR\):

\[s_{eff}=\frac{TPR}{FPR}\]

F1 and Flach’s G-measure

\[F_1=\frac{2TPR}{TPR+cFPR+1},\qquad G_F=\frac{TPR}{cFPR+1}\]

They are related by the strictly increasing transformation:

\[F_1=\frac{2G_F}{1+G_F}\]

Therefore they rank ROC points identically and have the same isometric geometry: they are skew-equivalent.

Weak versus strong skew-insensitivity

Strong: changing \(c\) changes neither values nor ordering; example \(TPR-FPR\).

Weak: values may be positively rescaled but ordering/isometric preference is preserved; example WRAcc.

Paper insights to remember

  • Accuracy has parallel isometrics with constant effective skew \(c\).
  • Precision has rays through the origin and varying effective skew over ROC space.
  • F1 and G-measure have the same isometrics but different numerical scales.
  • WRAcc isometrically behaves like the skew-insensitive core \(TPR-FPR\).
  • Nonlinear decision-tree criteria can have curved isometrics and point-dependent local slopes.

9. Probabilistic and information-theoretic metrics

Log-loss and cross-entropy

For binary classification:

\[ LL=-\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n\left[y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\right]. \]

This is empirical binary cross-entropy. In multiclass classification:

\[ CE=-\frac1n\sum_i\sum_k y_{ik}\log p_{ik} =-\frac1n\sum_i\log p_{i,\text{true class}}. \]

Exam identity: in standard supervised classification, log-loss and cross-entropy loss are the same quantity.

Brier score

\[ BS=\frac1n\sum_i(p_i-y_i)^2. \]

For binary \(y_i\in\{0,1\}\), Brier score is exactly MSE applied to predicted probabilities. Probability RMSE is \(\sqrt{BS}\).

Entropy, cross-entropy and KL divergence

\[ H(P)=-\sum_yP(y)\log P(y) \]

\[ H(P,Q)=-\sum_yP(y)\log Q(y) \]

\[ D_{KL}(P\|Q)=\sum_yP(y)\log\frac{P(y)}{Q(y)} \]

Always identify the distributions:
\(P\) is the target/reference/true distribution.
\(Q\) is the model-predicted or approximating distribution.

The relationship is:

\[ H(P,Q)=H(P)+D_{KL}(P\|Q). \]

\(H(P)\) is irreducible uncertainty in the target. KL is the extra penalty caused by using imperfect \(Q\).

Information score

A simple information contribution for the realized class is:

\[ I=\log\frac{P(y\mid output)}{P(y)}. \]

Example: prior probability of disease is \(0.10\), the model posterior is \(0.80\), and disease is truly present:

\[ I=\log_2\frac{0.80}{0.10}=\log_2 8=3\text{ bits}. \]

The model made the correct class eight times more plausible than the prior did. If the posterior were \(0.05\), the score would be \(-1\) bit: the model reduced belief in the class that was actually correct.

Calibration versus discrimination

Calibration asks whether predictions such as \(0.7\) correspond to approximately 70% positive frequency. Discrimination/ranking asks whether positives receive higher scores than negatives. A model can be good at one and poor at the other.

10. Agreement, subgroup discovery, efficiency and alternate measures

Chance-corrected agreement

\[ \kappa=\frac{p_o-p_e}{1-p_e} \]

\(p_o\) is observed agreement; \(p_e\) is agreement expected from marginal label frequencies. Fleiss’ kappa generalizes to multiple raters; ICC is commonly used for quantitative ratings.

Rule learning versus subgroup discovery

Rule learning tries to predict the target for cases, for example: “if fever is high and oxygen is low, predict severe disease.”

Subgroup discovery searches for interpretable subsets whose target distribution is unusual, for example: “patients who regularly consume alcohol and have feature pattern X show an unusually high cancer rate.” The subgroup need not classify everyone.

WRAcc

\[ WRAcc=P(subgroup)\left[P(+\mid subgroup)-P(+)\right]. \]

It rewards both:

  • enrichment: the subgroup has a higher positive rate than the population baseline;
  • coverage: the subgroup is large enough to matter.

In Flach’s ROC form:

\[ WRAcc=\frac{c}{(1+c)^2}(TPR-FPR) \]

possibly multiplied by \(4\) for rescaling. WRAcc was designed mainly for subgroup discovery, not primarily as a cost metric.

Efficiency method and Pareto analysis

The tutorial’s efficiency framework combines positive measures, where larger is better, with negative measures, where smaller is better:

\[ \varepsilon_S(f)= \frac{\sum_iw_i\,pmi_i^+(f)} {\sum_jw_j\,nm_j^-(f)}. \]

Examples of positive metrics: accuracy, interestingness, comprehensibility. Examples of negative metrics: runtime, memory, monetary cost.

A model is Pareto-optimal if no other model is at least as good on every objective and strictly better on at least one. The Pareto frontier contains all nondominated trade-offs.

Linear programming

Linear programming optimizes a linear objective under linear constraints:

\[ \max_x c^\top x\quad\text{subject to }Ax\le b. \]

It can be used for resource allocation, scheduling, or selecting weights subject to stated preference constraints.

Visualization-based evaluation

Scalar metrics are projections of richer, high-dimensional model behaviour. A model can be represented by its performance or error profile across datasets, classes, subgroups or instances. PCA, multidimensional scaling, heatmaps and clustering can reveal:

  • models with identical averages but different failure patterns;
  • unusual model–domain interactions;
  • outlier classifiers or datasets;
  • structure lost by averaging binary and multiclass domains into one scalar.

11. Common exam traps

  1. High accuracy does not imply useful minority-class performance.
  2. Recall and precision have different denominators.
  3. ROC point = one threshold; ROC curve = all thresholds.
  4. AUC does not evaluate probability calibration.
  5. AUC is better described as a ranking probability than as a simple average over costs.
  6. Small FPR may still mean many false alerts when negatives are enormous.
  7. F1 ignores TN.
  8. Balanced accuracy is not a universal cost-sensitive measure.
  9. Metric choice can change classifier ranking.
  10. Flach’s effective skew is the slope of an isometric, not \(\partial M/\partial c\).

12. Exam-style questions

Q1. A classifier has 99% accuracy on a 1% positive dataset. What must you inspect next?

Confusion matrix, recall, precision, specificity/FPR, balanced accuracy, and possibly PR/ROC. Accuracy alone may come from always predicting negative.

Q2. Why can FPR look good while precision is poor?

FPR divides FP by all negatives. With huge \(N\), many FP can still be a small fraction. Precision directly compares TP with FP among alerts.

Q3. What does AUC=0.8 mean?

A random positive is ranked above a random negative with probability approximately 0.8.

Q4. Why might a cancer-screening threshold be lower than 0.5?

Because FN cost is high; lowering the threshold raises sensitivity and lowers FNR, at the expense of more FP.

Q5. Explain Flach’s effective-skew ratio.

It is the slope of an equal-metric contour in ROC space: the local TPR gain needed to compensate for an FPR increase while keeping the metric constant.

Q6. Why may two metrics choose different classifiers?

They encode different denominators, error trade-offs, prevalence dependence, costs, or probability information.
