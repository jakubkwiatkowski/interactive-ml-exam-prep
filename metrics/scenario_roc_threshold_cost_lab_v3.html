How the best point is chosen

Each threshold gives one ROC point. A cost isometric has slope \[ \frac{P(+)C_{FN}}{P(-)C_{FP}}. \] Slide that line toward the upper-left. The last attainable contact point is the threshold with minimum expected cost. At a smooth interior optimum, the ROC tangent and the isometric have the same slope.

How to compute the optimal threshold efficiently

Empirical data: the ROC curve changes only when the threshold crosses a distinct score. Sort scores once from highest to lowest, update TP/FP while sweeping, and evaluate every distinct threshold.

For equal prevalence and equal costs:

\[ t^*=\arg\max_t\{TPR(t)-FPR(t)\} \]

This is Youden's \(J\). You still scan the ROC points, but the scan is linear after sorting.

For unequal prevalence or costs:

\[ t^*=\arg\min_t\left[P(+)C_{FN}FNR(t)+P(-)C_{FP}FPR(t)\right] \]

The practical algorithm is again a single scan over distinct thresholds. There is generally no need to compute a derivative, because an empirical ROC is a staircase with corners and ties.

Smooth theoretical case: if the population ROC is differentiable, an interior optimum satisfies:

\[ \frac{dTPR}{dFPR}=\frac{P(-)C_{FP}}{P(+)C_{FN}} \]

This tangency equation is a characterization of the optimum, not usually the fastest empirical implementation.

Cost and threshold — intuitive guide

Symbols
\(P(+)\): prevalence of positives, the fraction of real positive cases.
\(P(-)=1-P(+)\): prevalence of negatives.
\(C_{FN}\): cost of missing one positive.
\(C_{FP}\): cost of one false alarm.

Expected cost
\[ EC=P(+)C_{FN}FNR+P(-)C_{FP}FPR. \] The first term is the expected loss from missed positives. The second is the expected loss from false alarms.

Cost isometric
All ROC points with the same expected cost lie on one straight line. Its slope is \[ rac{P(+)C_{FN}}{P(-)C_{FP}}. \] A large slope means missed positives dominate; a small slope means false alarms dominate.

How to choose a threshold
Evaluate every distinct score threshold, compute its ROC point and expected cost, and choose the threshold with the smallest cost. Geometrically, slide a parallel cost line toward the upper-left until it last touches the ROC curve or convex hull.
