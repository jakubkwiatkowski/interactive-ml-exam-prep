Summary

The central relationship: sampling creates the evidence that testing analyzes

A significance test never sees the unknown population directly. It sees outputs produced by an evaluation design. Therefore, the validity of the test depends on what those outputs represent and whether they satisfy the test’s assumptions.

1Scientific question

Is one classifier better? Do any algorithms differ? Is a new method better than a control?

2Evaluation design

One dataset or many? Fixed test set or repeated resampling? Two classifiers or many?

3Error estimation

Hold-out, k-fold CV, repeated CV, bootstrap, 5×2cv, fixed test set.

4Statistical unit

One example? One split? One complete dataset/domain?

5Null model

Equal disagreement rates? Zero mean difference? Equal win probability? Equal ranks?

6Decision

Compute statistic and p-value or compare to a critical value; reject or fail to reject.

Golden exam question: What is one observation for this test? Until you answer that precisely, you do not yet know which significance test is valid.
One fixed test set

Paired predictions on individual examples → McNemar.

One dataset with repeated splits

Repeated performance differences with dependence → corrected resampled t or 5×2cv.

Many datasets

One reliable score per dataset per classifier → Wilcoxon or Friedman family.

McNemar test

NON-PARAMETRIC2 CLASSIFIERS1 DATASETPAIRED EXAMPLESDISAGREEMENT COUNTS
Use whenTwo classifiers are evaluated on the same fixed test examples from one practical dataset.
One observationOne test example (patient, image, email, row).
Works onBinary paired correctness outcomes, not numerical accuracies.
Null hypothesis\(H_0: p_{01}=p_{10}\), equivalently the two disagreement directions are equally likely.
StatisticContinuity-corrected \(\chi^2\) statistic, or exact binomial McNemar for small disagreement counts.
Reject when\(p<\alpha\), or \(\chi^2_{Mc}>\chi^2_{1,1-\alpha}\).
McNemar ignores examples where both classifiers agree. It asks only: among examples where they disagree, does one classifier win much more often?
Classical analogy

The same person tested before and after treatment; compare the two discordant response directions.

Classifier analogy

The same image is classified by A and B. Count A-wrong/B-correct versus A-correct/B-wrong.

Paired t-test

PARAMETRIC2 PAIRED SAMPLESRAW NUMERICAL DIFFERENCESMEAN DIFFERENCE
Use whenPaired numerical performance observations and the scientific target is the mean difference.
One observationOne paired difference \(d_i\). Its meaning depends on the design: one repeated run or one dataset-level difference.
Null hypothesis\(H_0:\mu_d=0\).
StatisticStudent t statistic with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.
Reject whenTwo-sided: \(|t|>t_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}\), equivalently \(p<\alpha\).
The t-statistic is a signal-to-noise ratio: observed mean difference divided by the standard error of that mean difference.
\[t=\frac{\bar d-0}{s_d/\sqrt n}\]
Cohen-like paired effect size

\(d_z=\bar d/s_d\): magnitude in SD units.

Relationship

For this paired version, \(t=d_z\sqrt n\). Increasing \(n\) can make a small effect statistically significant.

Key assumptions: paired observations; independent pairs; approximately normal distribution of differences for small n; meaningful numerical differences.
Naive CV warning: ordinary k-fold scores are not safely independent because training sets overlap. A plain paired t-test over folds can underestimate variance and inflate Type I error.
Across datasets: even if independence is plausible, raw performance differences may not be commensurable across very different domains. This is one reason Demšar recommends Wilcoxon.

Corrected resampled t-test

PARAMETRIC2 CLASSIFIERS1 DATASETREPEATED SPLITSDEPENDENCE CORRECTION
Use whenTwo classifiers are evaluated on one dataset using repeated random train/test splits.
One observationOne split-level performance difference, but observations are dependent due to overlap.
Null hypothesisExpected mean performance difference is zero.
Main ideaInflate the variance relative to a naive paired t-test to account for overlap.
Reject whenCorrected \(|t|\) exceeds its critical value or corrected \(p<\alpha\).
\[t_{corr}=\frac{\bar d}{\sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{r}+\frac{n_{test}}{n_{train}}\right)s_d^2}}\]
The exact notation varies by presentation, but the idea is stable: repeated resampling creates correlation; the denominator must be larger than the naive \(s_d/\sqrt r\).
Why useful

Safer than pretending repeated random splits are independent.

Why not Demšar’s main test

It addresses one dataset and repeated resampling, not generalization across many independent datasets.

5×2 cross-validation t/F tests

PARAMETRIC FAMILY2 CLASSIFIERS1 DATASET5 REPEATED 2-FOLD CVS
Use whenComparing two learning algorithms on one dataset with a resampling design built specifically for testing.
DataTen differences: two folds in each of five repetitions.
Null hypothesisThe algorithms have equal expected performance on this domain.
Why 2-fold?The two folds in each repetition swap train/test roles, creating a variance estimate tailored to the comparison.
Reject whenThe special t or F statistic exceeds its reference critical value, equivalently p<α.
RepetitionFold 1 differenceFold 2 differenceWithin-repetition meanVariance contribution
This test is inseparable from its resampling design. It is not “ordinary 10-fold CV with a t-test.”

Sign test

NON-PARAMETRIC2 CLASSIFIERSMULTIPLE DATASETSSIGNS / WINS ONLY
One observationOne dataset/domain.
InputFor each dataset: A wins, B wins, or tie.
Null hypothesis\(H_0:P(B\text{ wins})=0.5\).
StatisticNumber of wins; exact binomial reference distribution.
Reject whenTwo-sided binomial p-value < α, or wins exceed the exact critical count.
Under the null, each independent dataset acts like a fair coin flip: either classifier is equally likely to win.

Strength: almost no assumptions about numerical scale; only direction matters.
Weakness: ignores magnitude. A tiny win and a huge win count equally.
Exam trap: one Bernoulli trial is one dataset, not one example and not one CV fold.

Wilcoxon signed-rank test

NON-PARAMETRIC2 CLASSIFIERSMULTIPLE DATASETSSIGNED RANKSDEMŠAR RECOMMENDED
One observationOne dataset-level paired performance difference.
Input\(d_i=score_i(B)-score_i(A)\) for each dataset.
Null hypothesisSigned differences are centered/balanced around zero; no systematic rank advantage.
Statistic\(T=\min(R^+,R^-)\), where ranks come from \(|d_i|\).
Reject whenExact p<α, or T is at/below the exact critical value; for large N use normal approximation.
The sign test asks “who wins more often?” Wilcoxon asks “are the larger differences mostly in one direction?”

Repeated-measures ANOVA

PARAMETRICMANY CLASSIFIERSMULTIPLE DATASETSRAW SCORESOMNIBUS
One observationOne dataset with repeated measurements from all classifiers.
Null hypothesisAll classifier mean performances are equal.
StatisticF ratio: between-classifier variability divided by residual variability.
Reject when\(F>F_{df_1,df_2,1-\alpha}\), equivalently p<α.
Why often avoidedNormality and sphericity-type assumptions are doubtful in benchmark comparisons.
Demšar argues that repeated-measures ANOVA assumptions are often unsafe for classifier scores across heterogeneous datasets, especially sphericity.

Friedman test

NON-PARAMETRICMANY CLASSIFIERSMULTIPLE DATASETSWITHIN-DATASET RANKSOMNIBUS · DEMŠAR RECOMMENDED
One observationOne dataset with a rank assigned to every classifier.
Null hypothesisAll algorithms are equivalent, so expected average ranks are equal.
StatisticFriedman \(\chi_F^2\); often transformed to Iman–Davenport \(F_F\).
Reject whenp<α or statistic exceeds the reference critical value.
After rejectionUse post-hoc tests; Friedman alone does not identify the differing pairs.
Within each dataset, rank the algorithms. If all are equivalent, average ranks should be close to \((k+1)/2\). Persistent rank differences argue against the null.
\[\chi_F^2=\frac{12N}{k(k+1)}\left[\sum_{j=1}^k R_j^2-\frac{k(k+1)^2}{4}\right]\]
\[F_F=\frac{(N-1)\chi_F^2}{N(k-1)-\chi_F^2}\]
χ² Friedman–
Iman–Davenport F–
Approx. p-value–
Expected rank–
Why appropriate: multiple classifiers are measured on the same datasets, so ranking within each dataset handles scale differences more robustly.
Assumption: datasets are the independent blocks. Folds inside datasets are not the sample for Friedman.
Exam trap: significant Friedman means at least one difference exists, not that the top-ranked method beats every other method.
