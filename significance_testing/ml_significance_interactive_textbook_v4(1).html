The central relationship: sampling creates the evidence that testing analyzes

A significance test never sees the unknown population directly. It sees outputs produced by an evaluation design. Therefore, the validity of the test depends on what those outputs represent and whether they satisfy the test’s assumptions.

Is one classifier better? Do any algorithms differ? Is a new method better than a control?

2Evaluation design

One dataset or many? Fixed test set or repeated resampling? Two classifiers or many?

3Error estimation

Hold-out, k-fold CV, repeated CV, bootstrap, 5×2cv, fixed test set.

4Statistical unit

One example? One split? One complete dataset/domain?

5Null model

Equal disagreement rates? Zero mean difference? Equal win probability? Equal ranks?

6Decision

Compute statistic and p-value or compare to a critical value; reject or fail to reject.

Definitions and notation you should know

These words occur throughout the tutorial and Demšar paper. They describe different parts of the testing machinery, not interchangeable labels.

Parametric test

Uses a model with a finite set of parameters and stronger distributional assumptions. Example: paired t-test targets the mean difference and uses a t reference distribution.

Non-parametric test

Does not require a specific parametric form such as normality. It often works with signs, counts, or ranks. “Non-parametric” does not mean assumption-free.

Robustness

A procedure is robust when its conclusions change relatively little under moderate violations of assumptions, outliers, or small perturbations of the data. Robust does not mean universally correct; it means less sensitive to particular deviations than an alternative procedure.

Omnibus test

A global test for more than two methods. It asks whether any difference exists somewhere. ANOVA and Friedman are omnibus tests; they do not identify the differing pair.

Post-hoc test

A follow-up after an omnibus rejection. Tukey follows ANOVA; Nemenyi follows Friedman. Control-focused means that one method is declared in advance as the reference—usually the new algorithm or a standard baseline—and only comparisons control-vs-A, control-vs-B, … are tested. Comparisons among the non-control methods are deliberately omitted, which reduces the number of hypotheses and usually improves power.

i.i.d.

Independent and identically distributed. Independent means one observation does not reveal or determine another. Identically distributed means they arise from the same probability mechanism.

Paired design

Both algorithms are evaluated under the same conditions: same test instances, same split, or same dataset. Pairing removes irrelevant between-condition variation.

Statistical unit

The object counted as one observation by the test: an example for McNemar, a repeated run for a resampling test, or a dataset for Demšar-style Wilcoxon/Friedman.

Commensurability

Whether numerical differences can be placed on one common additive scale. Across domains, +0.02 accuracy can have very different practical meaning because baseline difficulty, class balance, metric range, and remaining reducible error differ. The issue can also occur within one domain: improving 0.50→0.52 and 0.98→1.00 are both +0.02, but the second removes all remaining error. For one fixed dataset, paired differences are still numerically comparable because they concern the same task and metric, but practical importance may remain nonlinear. Alternatives include error reduction, relative change, log-odds, utility/cost measures, or reporting raw differences beside rank-based tests.

One-sided vs two-sided

Two-sided asks whether algorithms differ in either direction. One-sided asks whether a pre-specified algorithm is better. The direction must be chosen before seeing the results.

Family-wise error rate

The probability of at least one Type I error in a family of comparisons. This is why many pairwise tests need Tukey, Nemenyi, Holm, or another correction.

Post-hoc labels: Tukey is a parametric ANOVA follow-up; Nemenyi is a rank-based Friedman follow-up. Holm and Bonferroni are general multiple-testing corrections—their parametric/non-parametric character comes from the underlying p-values, not from Holm itself.

General trade-off: stronger assumptions let a test exploit more numerical information and can yield more power when correct. P-value laboratory: the same logic for discrete and continuous tests

A p-value is the probability, calculated under the null model, of obtaining a test statistic at least as incompatible with the null as the observed statistic. “At least as extreme” is defined by the chosen alternative and test statistic.

Continuous reference distribution

For a two-sided t-test, \(p=P(|T|\ge |t_{obs}|\mid H_0)\). Individual exact points have probability zero; areas over ranges carry probability.

Discrete reference distribution

For a sign test, \(X\sim Binomial(N,0.5)\). Probabilities sit on integer outcomes. The exact p-value sums outcomes at least as extreme as the observed win count.

\[p_{\text{two-sided}}=P\!\left(\lvert T\rvert\ge \lvert t_{\mathrm{obs}}\rvert\mid H_0\right)\]
\[P(X=x\mid H_0)=\binom{N}{x}(0.5)^x(0.5)^{N-x}=\binom{N}{x}2^{-N}\]
Not: \(P(H_0\mid data)\). A p-value does not tell you the probability that the null is true, nor the probability that the result will replicate.
p-value route

Compute the observed statistic, convert it to a tail probability under \(H_0\), and reject when \(p\le\alpha\).

critical-value route

Convert \(\alpha\) into rejection boundary/boundaries and check whether the observed statistic falls in the rejection region. For right-tailed statistics such as \(\chi^2\) and \(F\), this is usually “statistic > critical value.” For two-sided t/z tests use \(|t|>t_{crit}\). For left-tailed tests reject for a sufficiently small statistic. Some statistics, such as the traditional Wilcoxon \(T=\min(R^+,R^-)\), reject for a small value.

Classical statistics ↔ machine-learning translation map

Most of these tests were not invented specifically for machine learning. The easiest way to remember them is to map the classical experimental unit, treatment, and response to their ML counterparts. The mapping changes with the evaluation design, so “patient = dataset” is true in a multiple-domain benchmark, but not in McNemar’s fixed-test-set design.

Test-by-test analogy

McNemar

Classical: the same person gives a binary response before and after treatment, or two diagnostic methods classify the same patient. ML: classifier A and B predict the same test example. One case is one example; count the two discordant directions.

Paired t-test

Classical: blood pressure before vs after treatment for each person. ML: paired performance values from the same independent repetition, split, or dataset. One observation is one paired numerical difference.

Sign test

Classical: for each subject/block, did treatment B improve the response relative to A? Use only +/−. ML: for each dataset, did B beat A? One Bernoulli trial is one dataset-level winner.

Wilcoxon signed-rank

Classical: rank the absolute before/after changes for patients and restore signs. ML: rank the absolute classifier-performance differences across datasets and restore which classifier won.

Ordinary one-way ANOVA

Classical: different independent groups receive different treatments. ML analogue: would require separate independent datasets/units assigned to different classifiers, which is usually wasteful and not the standard benchmark design.

Repeated-measures ANOVA

Classical: every patient receives every treatment. ML: every dataset is evaluated by every classifier. Dataset is the subject/block; classifier is the treatment.

Friedman

The rank-based analogue of repeated-measures ANOVA. Every dataset/block receives all classifiers/treatments, but the analysis uses within-dataset ranks instead of raw responses.

Post-hoc procedures

Classical: after finding that treatments differ overall, determine which treatment pairs differ. ML: after ANOVA/Friedman, compare classifier pairs with Tukey/Nemenyi or compare each method only with a chosen control using Dunnett/Holm-type procedures.

Do not memorize one universal mapping. In McNemar, the “subject” is one test instance. In a Demšar comparison, the “subject/block” is one dataset. In repeated-resampling tests, the observation is a repetition/split. The statistical unit follows the design.

Ordinary one-way ANOVA vs one-way repeated-measures ANOVA

Both compare more than two condition means, but they differ in whether the measurements in different conditions come from independent subjects or from the same subjects/blocks. That difference changes the model, the decomposition of variability, and the appropriate ML analogy.

Ordinary one-way ANOVA: independent groups

Group A contains different people from Group B and Group C. Each subject receives only one treatment. The observations between groups are independent.

Repeated-measures ANOVA: related measurements

The same person is measured under A, B, and C, or matched blocks are exposed to every condition. Subject-level baselines are modeled explicitly.

Variance decomposition

Ordinary one-way ANOVA
\[SS_{\mathrm{Total}}=SS_{\mathrm{Between\ groups}}+SS_{\mathrm{Within\ groups}}\]

The within-group term contains both individual differences and unexplained noise because different subjects occur in different groups.

One-way repeated-measures ANOVA
\[SS_{\mathrm{Total}}=SS_{\mathrm{Classifier}}+SS_{\mathrm{Dataset}}+SS_{\mathrm{Residual}}\]

The dataset/block term removes stable difficulty differences. The residual largely represents classifier×dataset interaction and other unexplained variation.

\[F=\frac{MS_{\mathrm{Classifier}}}{MS_{\mathrm{Residual}}}\]
Core intuition: Dataset 1 may be easy for every algorithm and Dataset 2 difficult for every algorithm. Repeated-measures ANOVA removes that common dataset difficulty from the error term. It then asks whether systematic classifier differences are large relative to the remaining classifier-by-dataset variability.

Choose direction before inspecting results. Under a symmetric null, each tail has probability \(\alpha/2\) in a two-sided test. If you inspect the sign first and then choose whichever one-sided tail is favorable, you effectively allow either tail to trigger rejection while still pretending you allocated only \(\alpha\) to one tail. The total false-positive opportunity is therefore about \(2\alpha\).
Key assumptions: paired observations; independent pairs; approximately normal distribution of differences for small n; meaningful numerical differences. For the paired test, normality concerns the distribution of the paired differences \(d_i\), not necessarily each classifier’s score separately.
Naive CV warning: ordinary k-fold scores are not safely independent because training sets overlap. A plain paired t-test over folds can underestimate variance and inflate Type I error.
Across datasets: even if independence is plausible, raw performance differences may not be commensurable across very different domains. This is one reason Demšar recommends Wilcoxon.
When paired t can be good: if every pair comes from a genuinely independent replicated experiment (fresh train sample and fresh test sample), the same pairing is used for A and B, differences are approximately normal, and the numerical scale is meaningful. One single hold-out gives only one aggregate accuracy difference, so it does not provide a sample of differences for a t-test.

What about ties?

Common exact practice removes ties and reduces N. Demšar’s presentation also discusses splitting ties evenly for win-count summaries; always state the convention.

They are the same test. The table lists the minimum win count needed at a chosen α; the p-value computes the exact tail probability for your observed count.

Normality: residuals/differences should be approximately normal. With a small number of datasets, this is hard to verify and outliers matter.
Sphericity: variances of every pairwise classifier difference should be equal. In benchmark studies, A vs B may be very stable while A vs C varies dramatically by domain, so this assumption is implausible. Violation makes the F reference distribution too optimistic unless corrected.
Heterogeneous domains: raw score differences must be meaningful across datasets. Accuracy bounded near 1 on easy datasets and lower on hard datasets creates non-uniform variance and interactions.
Why Friedman is safer: ranking within each dataset removes raw-scale differences and avoids normality/sphericity assumptions, though it discards magnitude information.

Methods connected by the same horizontal bar are not significantly distinguishable at the chosen α. This does not prove equivalence; it means the experiment lacks enough evidence to separate them.

Important conditionThese inherit ANOVA’s normality, variance/sphericity, and raw-scale assumptions.

Tukey: all-vs-all

Controls family-wise error over all \(k(k-1)/2\) pairs. Parametric analogue of the all-pairs role served by Nemenyi after Friedman.

Dunnett: control-vs-rest

Uses only \(k-1\) comparisons and is more powerful when the scientific question centers on a new method versus baselines.

Do not mix branches: Tukey follows ANOVA/raw means; Nemenyi follows Friedman/ranks. Holm can adjust p-values from many different underlying tests.

Control-vs-rest post-hoc: Bonferroni-Dunn and Holm

Meaning of “control”: select one classifier before looking at the results—for example, the proposed method or a standard baseline. Test only Control vs A, Control vs B, Control vs C. You do not test A vs B, A vs C, or B vs C unless those comparisons are also part of the research question.

High-value exam insights gathered from the tutorial and Demšar paper

1. NHST is support, not proofRejecting at α=.05 does not mean a 95% probability that the alternative is true.
2. Significance ≠ importanceWith enough observations, tiny effects can be significant. Report effect size and actual score differences.
3. Test choice defines the precise nullMean difference, win probability, disagreement symmetry, and equal ranks are related but not identical questions.
4. Use the same splitsPairing algorithms on the same partitions removes split-to-split noise and improves comparison efficiency.
5. Do not count significant wins onlyDemšar explains that thresholding every domain at p<.05 discards consistent weak evidence and creates a misleading two-stage procedure.
6. Dataset selection mattersA statistical test generalizes only to the population of domains represented by the chosen benchmark suite.
7. More datasets improve powerFor Wilcoxon/Friedman, N is the number of datasets. More appropriate independent domains are usually more valuable than more overlapping folds.
8. Omnibus then post-hocFriedman/ANOVA first; then choose all-vs-all or control-vs-rest according to the scientific question.
9. Non-rejection is not equalityIt may reflect low power, few datasets, noisy estimates, or a conservative post-hoc correction.
10. Rank methods trade magnitude for robustnessWilcoxon/Friedman reduce outlier and scale sensitivity, but they do not preserve every numerical detail.
